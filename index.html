Read the statement, not the person

Veritas doesn't watch faces or measure a pulse. It works entirely from text — transcripts, chat logs, written statements — scoring each claim against patterns linked to deceptive language in the research literature.

Qualifier overuse, low self-reference, verb-tense shifts, and detail that's either suspiciously thin or suspiciously rehearsed — each is weighed on its own terms, not folded into a single gut impression of the whole passage.

A field guide to the markers

Exhibit — Three specimens

i.

Hedging

"Kind of," "I guess," "give or take" — qualifiers standing in for certainty.

ii.

Distancing

Fewer first-person pronouns, more passive framing — the narrator steps back.

iii.

Tense drift

Slipping between past and present mid-account — a seam in the timeline.

Method

Three passes over every statement

01

Statement intake

Raw text is split into individual claims so each one is scored on its own, not as part of a general impression of the whole passage.

02

Linguistic scan

Each claim is checked against known deception markers: hedges, distancing pronouns, tense inconsistency, and detail that's over- or under-specified.

03

Confidence score

Flagged claims are weighted into a single 0–100 score, with the exact phrases that moved the needle highlighted in the output.

Marginalia

From the literature this leans on

Liars tend to distance themselves from their account — fewer first-person pronouns, more passive framing.

Undeutsch hypothesis

Deceptive accounts often carry either too little sensory detail, or too much of the wrong kind.

Reality monitoring theory

Cognitive load from fabrication shows up as hedging — qualifiers doing the work certainty can't.

Verbal cue research

Where this breaks

  • i.

    Text only. No tone of voice, no context outside the statement itself — a flat, factual account can read as suspicious for having none of the hedges a nervous truth-teller might use.

  • ii.

    English-trained. Marker patterns were tuned on English statement corpora; accuracy on other languages and heavy dialect is unverified.

  • iii.

    Score, not proof. A high score means the language pattern resembles deceptive statements in training data — a lead to check, not a finding.
